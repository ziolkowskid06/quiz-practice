  - id: 2
    type: multiple-choice
    question: "Two team members have been deadlocked for days over a design decision and neither will concede. Time is running out. Which conflict technique should the PM use?"
    options: ["Collaborate/Problem-Solve", "Withdraw/Avoid", "Compromise/Reconcile", "Smooth/Accommodate"]
    answer: "Compromise/Reconcile"
    explanation: "Compromise/Reconcile is appropriate when both parties have equal power, are entirely deadlocked, and time pressure demands a decision. Both parties give up something to move forward. While not the PMI Gold Standard, it is the pragmatic choice when Collaborate is no longer feasible given time constraints."

    options: ["Collaborate/Problem-Solve", "Compromise/Reconcile", "Force/Direct", "Smooth/Accommodate"]
    answer: "Force/Direct"
    explanation: "Force/Direct is appropriate in emergencies requiring immediate action, or when a non-negotiable policy (safety, legal compliance) must be enforced. In a safety violation scenario, you do not collaborate — you direct the person to stop immediately. This is one of the rare PMI-approved uses of Force."

  - id: 11
    type: multiple-choice
    question: "A PM's team has just come together for a new project. Everyone is polite but uncertain about their roles and how decisions will be made. What leadership style is most appropriate right now?"
    options: ["Delegative", "Participative", "Directive", "Coaching"]
    answer: "Directive"
    explanation: "In the Forming stage, the team lacks a shared understanding of goals, roles, and ways of working. The PM must be highly Directive — providing extreme clarity on the project vision, establishing ground rules, and structuring the early work. Stepping back too soon (Participative or Delegative) will leave the team adrift."

  - id: 12
    type: multiple-choice

    options: ["Force a decision immediately to stop the disruption", "Withdraw until tensions cool permanently", "Coach and facilitate the team to resolve the root cause using ground rules", "Reassign the conflicting engineers to different projects"]
    answer: "Coach and facilitate the team to resolve the root cause using ground rules"
    explanation: "In the Storming stage, the PM shifts to Coach and Facilitator. Storming is natural and necessary — suppressing it with Force or ignoring it with Withdraw will either damage morale or let the conflict fester. The PMI answer is always to diagnose the root cause and use the Collaborate/Problem-Solve technique with the team's own ground rules."

  - id: 14
    type: fill-blank

    answer: "Adjourning"
    accept: ["adjourning", "mourning", "Mourning"]
    explanation: "Tuckman originally published Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing in 1965. He added Adjourning (also called Mourning) in 1977 to capture the emotional reality of team dissolution. The PM's role here is Closure: capturing lessons learned, celebrating achievements, and helping team members transition to their next roles."

  - id: 25
    type: multiple-choice
    question: "Layoff rumors are spreading and your Agile team stops engaging with Sprint work. You try to motivate them by emphasizing how 'creatively fulfilling' the next Sprint will be. According to Maslow, why is this failing?"
    options: ["Their Esteem needs are unmet", "Their Safety and Security needs are threatened", "They lack self-actualization opportunities", "The Hygiene factors are insufficient"]
    answer: "Their Safety and Security needs are threatened"
    explanation: "Maslow's Hierarchy requires that lower-level needs be met before higher ones can motivate. Safety and Security (job security) sits below Esteem and Self-Actualization. When layoffs threaten job security, talking about 'creative fulfillment' (a Self-Actualization need) falls completely flat. The PM must address the foundational fear first."

  - id: 26

    options: ["Increase the bonus amount to make it more compelling", "Add overtime pay on top of the bonus", "Assign meaningful work with ownership and recognition", "Set clearer consequences for missing the deadline"]
    answer: "Assign meaningful work with ownership and recognition"
    explanation: "Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory: Hygiene factors (salary, bonuses) prevent dissatisfaction but do NOT create motivation. True Motivating Factors are recognition, challenging work, personal growth, and responsibility. Throwing money at a burned-out team temporarily stops them from quitting — it does not make them care about the project."

  - id: 28
    type: multiple-choice
    question: "An employee will only put in high effort if they believe: (1) effort will lead to good performance, (2) good performance will be rewarded, and (3) the reward matters to them. Which motivational theory describes this?"
    options: ["Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs", "Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory", "McClelland's Acquired Needs Theory", "Vroom's Expectancy Theory"]
    answer: "Vroom's Expectancy Theory"
    explanation: "Vroom's Expectancy Theory has three components: Expectancy (effort leads to performance), Instrumentality (performance leads to reward), and Valence (the reward is valued). If any one of the three breaks down — impossible goal, reward not delivered, or reward nobody cares about — motivation collapses entirely."
